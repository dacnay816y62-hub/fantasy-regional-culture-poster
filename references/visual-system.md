## Composition

Make the large character the composition, not a headline placed on top. It should carry region recognition, visual weight, spatial framework, and cultural translation.

Preferred baseline: one culturally condensed huge character fused with one cropped spatial or architectural mechanism. The viewer should remember the character-image collision first. Clean full-place-name posters are a fallback, not the default, when the single-character route cannot stay readable or specific.

Anti-rustic baseline: make the collision feel contemporary, not like a heritage-tourism board. Historical or local material can appear, but treat it through cool palette, abstraction, cropped structure, modern contrast, or material precision rather than nostalgia.

Text-removal recognition baseline: do not let the city name do all the cultural work. Mentally cover the city name, theme, keywords, and epigraph; the remaining image should still show at least one place-specific cultural mechanism. It does not need to be a famous landmark, but it must be hard to transplant to another city without becoming false.

Preferred structures:

- centered or near-symmetrical order
- strong vertical axis
- single-side compression
- radical crop
- slit, gate, wall-gap, or corridor composition
- foreground/background occlusion
- one broad quiet field interrupted by one dense structural zone
- abstract spatial section, material cut, aperture, threshold, infrastructural fragment, or compressed city-layer composition
- place-specific mechanisms such as arcade-river humidity, lane-house compression, city-wall aperture plus plane-tree shadow, kiln/workshop seams, garden-window water borrowing, cliff stair-bridge section, ice-brick winter street, loess cave-wall cut, rammed-earth tulou ring, or maritime temple-arcade layering

Use generous whitespace as an active spatial element. A poster is not complete merely because all available areas contain information.

## Character-image integration

Use no more than two or three integration operations per poster:

- masking
- negative space
- partial crop
- foreground occlusion
- stroke interruption
- nested architecture
- image contained within a stroke
- material transition across the glyph
- old/new layers aligned to different glyph planes

The character must remain identifiable. Decorative distortion without cultural evidence is prohibited.

## Image selection

Prefer:

- architectural fragments rather than complete monuments
- culturally specific fragments, processes, and spatial behaviors that remain recognizable without explanatory text
- material surfaces and weathering
- boundaries, thresholds, voids, and corridors
- roof, ridge, gable, wall, gate, courtyard, and alley structures
- paths, shadow, mist, water edge, and city fragments
- old/new overlap with clear hierarchy

Avoid:

- postcard landscape views
- city landmark panoramas
- centered heroic building facades
- movie-poster drama, commercial campaign polish, and cinematic spectacle
- heritage-tourism board mood, folk-culture promo texture, faux-ancient rubbing, dusty ochre nostalgia, old-wall-only backgrounds, and museum souvenir styling
- icon mosaics
- arbitrary traditional patterns
- crowded cultural inventories
- generic cinematic atmosphere that hides the actual place
- generic modern visual ingredients as the main evidence: glass wall, concrete plane, bridge, water reflection, fog, skyline, neon, and blank gallery wall are only supporting materials unless anchored to a local mechanism

## Color

Use one dominant color, one or two supporting colors, and one neutral. Derive the palette from climate, material, light, and cultural memory.

Reference tendencies, not fixed presets:

- 北京: palace-wall red, muted warm gold, deep shadow, rice white
- 广东: humid grey-blue, deep green, warm grey, dark ink, restrained urban light
- 福建: sea-fog grey, red brick, lime white, blue-grey
- 陕西: earth ochre, lime white, dark red, blue-grey
- 四川: smoke grey, ink green, dark red, mist white
- 上海: grey-white, black, metallic grey, dark wine red
- 江苏: white, grey, ink black, quiet timber
- 云南: earth red, mountain-mist grey, ink green, indigo
- 西藏: mineral white, rock grey, restrained Tibetan red, sky blue

Do not make “premium” synonymous with monochrome grey. Let the place supply the accent.

Do not make “regional culture” default to earth yellow, sepia, cracked plaster, dusty wall, fake rubbing, antique paper, red seal, or heavy stone inscription texture. Prefer contemporary palettes and materials when the user wants higher design: fog white, cool grey, deep green-black, wet concrete, glass reflection, metal edge, polished stone, abstract water plane, shadow cuts, and restrained electric or sodium accents.

## Typography

Use four levels:

1. dominant character
2. compact theme title
3. 3-5 keywords
4. one short epigraph

Chinese is primary. English, when used, is metadata and must not compete. Keep small text sparse, aligned, and rhythmically grouped. Use exact digital typesetting for Chinese rather than accepting model-generated glyphs.

The epigraph should be concrete, visual, and restrained. It must not tell the viewer to visit, discover, experience, or celebrate the region.

When the user prioritizes legibility, use the full place name as the dominant title. Keep it exact and instantly readable; put the experimental work into spatial logic, material texture, cropping, voids, apertures, and the relationship between title and image. Do not force character-building fusion if it makes the theme unreadable.

Use typography as the first place to add richness to an otherwise clean structure, but do not let typography replace the core spatial idea. Customize the dominant title through:

- stroke weight contrast and proportion
- serif, terminal, and corner treatment
- counter shape and internal spacing
- vertical or horizontal rhythm
- measured stroke cuts, gaps, or interruptions
- material edge behavior, such as stone-cut, porcelain glaze, lime-wall paint, ink seepage, frost bite, or worn inscription
- spacing and alignment that respond to the spatial structure

Keep these modifications typographic, not illustrative. A viewer should read the character or place name first, then notice the cultural logic in the letterform. If the title becomes hard to parse, reduce the customization before changing the composition. If the result feels too safe, strengthen the character-image mechanism before adding decorative detail.

## Negative rules

Reject:

- tourism-promotion aesthetics
- movie-poster or commercial-KV aesthetics
- heritage-tourism board aesthetics
- folk-culture promo, faux-ancient rubbing, dusty ochre nostalgia, old-wall-only background, museum souvenir styling
- landmark grids
- ornate red-and-gold decoration
- cartoon or souvenir styling
- slogan-like copy
- excess information
- safe full-place-name wall posters with no memorable spatial or cultural mechanism
- posters where covering the city name makes the place impossible to infer because the image only shows generic modern materials
- inconsistent materials
- “traditional versus modern” shown as a simplistic left/right split
- a fixed layout that only swaps region names
